Chapter 1 INTRODUCTION 
 
Wat Phra Mahathat Woramahawihan, Nakhon Si Thammarat is one of the most valuable historical 
remains in Thailand. This heritage site has possib le threats as the appearance and structural 
conditions have been deteriorating due to aging, environmental impacts and man-made activities. 
Proper maintenance and monitoring plan is therefore very important for preservation of the site. 
In order to have rational plan and decision, docume ntations and assessment of the existing 
conditions of the structures are necessary. In thi s research, the main objective is to apply 
engineering measures for documentations of the exis ting condition of Wat Phra Mahathat 
Woramahawihan. The obtained results will support monitoring schemes for the site to achieve a 
goal of sustainable preservation. 
 
The study begins with geometry survey of the struct ures using 3-dimensional digital 
documentation techniques. Two advanced techniques are employed in the study; the terrestrial 
laser scanning technique, as detailed in Chapter 2 and the image-based modeling technique, as 
detailed in Chapter 3. Dynamic tests of the chedis to identify their natural frequencies are 
explained in Chapter 4. Then, structural analysis of Chedi Phra Mahathat is performed in Chapter 
5 to examine the stability of the structure using 3D Finite Element Modelling constructed from the 
results in Chapter 2 to 4. Finally, effects of traffic induced vibration are examined in Chapter 6.

Chapter 4 IDENTIFICATION OF NATURAL FREQUENCY OF 
STRUCTURE BY AMBIENT VIBRATION TEST 
 
4.1 Fundamental of the Identification Technique 
Natural frequency is an important parameter of a structure as it composed of mass and elasticity 
information or natural properties of the structure. The natural frequency can be used to calibrate 
a finite element model for structural analysis. Moreover, it can be regularly monitored in order to 
detect any change in mass or elasticity of the stru cture which is beneficial for conservation and 
maintenance plan of an important structure. 
 
In general, natural frequencies of structures can b e calculated by numerical analysis of the 
structural model or identified by direct measurement of the actual structure. The numerical study 
yields complete results but likely to be inaccurate . In addition, effects of complex vibration 
characteristics as torsional eccentricity or founda tion compliance may not be correctly modeled. 
Thus, direct measurement through dynamic test is considered to provide more reliable results. 
There are two forms of dynamic test on structures, forced vibration test and ambient vibration 
test. The forced vibration test is to measure responses of the structure under artificially controlled 
excitation, while the ambient vibration test is to measure responses of the structure induced by 
wind, traffic loads nearby and other environmental forces. The ambient vibration test has been 
verified to be very practical yet yields accurate r esults; therefore, this study applies the ambient 
vibration test. This test requires processing signals for identifying dynamic properties of structures. 
The signal processing techniques can be categorized into time domain and frequency domain 
technique. In the time domain technique, response-t ime history is employed directly in the 
identification of dynamic properties of structure. While in the frequency domain technique, 
response-time history has to be firstly converted i nto frequency domain by Fourier analysis. 
Dynamic properties are then extracted from the freq uency spectrum, which is plotting of Fourier 
magnitude response against frequencies. Although bo th techniques can be employed to identify 
the dynamic properties of structures, the frequency domain technique gives a better physical 
interpretation than the time domain technique becau se it presents the response of structures in 
the form frequency spectrum. In this technique, the natural vibrations from ambient disturbances 
of a structure are recorded in time domain and transformed into frequency domain spectrum using 
Fast Fourier Transform technique. By assuming that excitations are stationary random process 
with a board band spectrum, the frequency spectrum can be used directly to identify natural 
frequencies from the frequencies corresponding to the peak values of the Fourier magnitude.

4.2 Measuring equipment 
The main equipment used in the measurement is a set of velocity meters to record the response 
of the structure in form of velocity of motion. The velocity meters consist of 3-axis velocity sensors, 
a portable recorder and a notebook computer as show n in Figures. 4.1. The velocity sensors 
convert the vibratory velocities into analog electrical signals. The recorder further converts these 
analog signals into equivalent digital signals and records them in its memory. The notebook 
computer is used for controlling the recorder through a special data acquisition software package. 
The resolution of the analog to digital converter i s 24 bits with maximum converting speed of 50 
kHz, and maximum input voltage of 2.5 V. The sensitivity is 1 V/(cm/s) and the frequency range 
is from 0.5 to 20 Hz. The system can measure velocity in the scale of micro-m/s and the maximum 
scale is ± 2.5 cm/s. 
 
 
Figure 4.1: Velocity measuring system

and 22 subsidiary chedis. The subsidiary chedis ar e smaller than Chedi Phra Mahathat, and

measurement. The number of the studied chedis in each area is, 6, 8 and 8 for area 1, 2 and 3,

5.2 Material properties 
It is assumed that the material is linear-elastic isotropic and homogeneous material. The important

elasticity (E). It is reported in [2] that the dry density of samples taken directly from Chedi Phra

3 to 1,860 kg/m 3, and with the average moisture content of 6% 
the average density is about 1,850 kg/m 3. Accordingly, with no further information, the mas s 
density in this study is assumed to be 1,850 kg/m 3. With the volume of 10,075 m 3, the total weight 
of the structure is approximated to be 182.84 MN. P oisson's ratio of the material plays a limited 
role in the analysis and a typical value of 0.2 is assumed. 
 
Stiffness of the structure depends directly on the modulus of elasticity of the material. With the 
assumption that the material is isotropic and homog eneous material and can be represented by

plays an important role on how the structure responses dynamically, it is possible to use dynamic 
properties of structure to estimate for the appropriate value of the modulus.

6.1 Introduction 
Vibration induced by traffic can be harmful for structures if its intensity exceeds certain level. This 
problem is more pronounced for the case of ancient or historic structures. The characteristics of 
vibration problem depend on several factors such as properties of soil media in which vibration 
waves propagate, vehicle types and speed, distance from source. In order to properly investigate 
the problem, field measurement at the site is neces sary. In this section, the results of an 
assessment of vibration induced by traffic at Wat P hra Mahathat Woramahawihan, Nakhon Si 
Thammarat are presented. The main objective is to assess the vibration effect on the historical 
structures in the temple by direct measurement of t he vibration intensity. The obtained results 
were compare with vibration criteria for historic structure. Moreover, an attenuation characteristics 
of the vibration wave are investigated in this site.

6.3 Site condition 
Characteristic of subsoils such as shear wave velocity profile along depth from surface at the site 
was explored by applying array microtremor measurem ent. The fundamental concept of array 
microtremor technique is to simultaneously record t he vertical component of microtremors for 
several positions in the survey area to obtain Rayl eigh wave samples propagating from a wide 
range of azimuthal angles. The coherency spectrum can then be computed for any pair of sensor 
in the array to evaluate the correlation among them and to determine phase velocity characteristic 
which is dispersive. From the dispersion relation of phase velocity and frequency, the results 
from field observations are then compared with thos e derived theoretically from a horizontally 
layered earth model by iteration procedure. The re sults of best-fit shear wave velocity–depth 
profile can be determined from the inversion analysis. Figure 6.4 show the arrangement of sensor 
for microtremor array measurement. Figure 6.5 pres ents the results of this survey as the 
dispersion curve and the shear wave velocity profile. As shown in Figure 6.5 (b), the shear wave

6.4 Attenuation of Vibration Wave

in all directions. The propagation characteristics of the wave depends mainly on characteristics

characteristic. In this study, attenuation rate of traveling wave from traffic source is investigated 
by field observation. The attenuation rate was con sidered as a ratio of the amplitudes between 
two sensors and the distance between them. In Figure 6.6, for example in the top left figure, the

detected at sensor number 2. The attenuation rate from these sensors is obtained from the slope 
of the data points which is simplified as a straight line. This example indicates that the amplitude 
at the second sensor reduces to be about 78% of the value at the first sensor. The results from 
others pairs are presented in the same form in this figure. Finally, the attenuation characteristic 
can be described in Figure 6.7 and 6.8 where the re sults from this study is compared with the

6.5 Vibration Intensity and Acceptance Criteria for Historic Buildings 
Generally, the recommended values of vibration acce ptance criteria for historic buildings are 
defined in serviceability limit states, which are t he limit of hairline or minor cracking, spalling of 
paint or plaster, excessive deflection or accelerat ed aging [2]. Most vibration criteria have been 
developed from experience and are therefore of an empirical in nature. The recommended values 
depend on type of excitation, frequency content, du ration as well as the position where the 
vibration is considered. The limits for historic b uildings are always much lower than those for 
other types of structures. The tolerable values vary from country to country and no single set of 
criteria seems to satisfy all requirements. It sho uld be noted that the recommended criteria do 
not guarantee absence of damage, but reduce its pro bability of occurrence to acceptably low 
levels.

Chapter 7 SUMMARY 
 
In this study, engineering measures were applied fo r documentation and assessment of the 
existing conditions of Wat Phra Mahathat Woramahawi han, Nakhon Si Thammarat. The major 
outputs from the study can be summarized as follows. 
 
1. Terrestrial laser scanning technology was used t o collect point cloud data for 3D digital 
documentation. In this study, seventy-four laser s canner stations were used to collect the point 
cloud data of the area, and approximately one billi on 3D point clouds of the Wat Phra Mahathat 
Woramahawihan were generated. Finally, point cloud data are stored and shared using Webshare 
application and they are accessible for monitoring in the future conservation plan. 
 
2. 3D digital documentation was also conducted by t he image-based photogrammetry technique 
to estimate the tilt angle and inspect the surface damage. The 3D model was also created for 
Finite Element modeling to evaluate stability of the structure. By comparing with the laser scanner, 
the 3D model from the image-based technique provides an identical result. It was also found that 
the top of the stupa was tilted by about 1.5 degree southward. This technique also provides a 
practical measure for inspection of surface damage. 
 
3. Ambient vibration tests were conducted to identi fy the natural frequencies of Chedi Phra 
Mahathat and 22 subsidiary chedis. The identified natural frequency for the first mode of vibration 
of Chedi Phra Mahathat is about 1.96 Hz. and 1.84 H z. In X and Y direction, respectively. The 
satellite chedi has the natural frequency for the f irst mode of vibration of about 10.5 Hz. The 
results of the identified natural periods of the fu ndamental mode and height of the subsidiary 
chedis are presented as the natural periods increase with height of the chedis. 
 
4. Finite element modeling of Chedi Phra Mahathat w as studied in order to perform structural 
analysis due to self-weight. The information from t he Structure from Motion technique and 3-D 
laser scan were used to define the geometry of the model. The elastic modulus was estimated to 
be 2,200 MPa such that the natural frequency of the model is equal to the measured value at 
1.90 Hz. The structure is in safe state with safet y factor greater than 2.0, and bearing stress at 
base is well below the soil bearing capacity. Moda l dynamic properties of the model for the first 
30 modes were reported. Effect of assumed tilting angle was also investigated.

5. Vibration induced from traffic was studied by fi eld measurement in order to assess its effects 
to the historical site. Site characteristic was fi rstly determined by microtremor observation, and 
the obtained average shear wave velocity from surface to 30 m depth was 272 m/s. Attenuation 
of vibration amplitude was examined and it was foun d that due to stiff subsoils the attenuation 
rate is faster than other soft soil areas. The hig hest vibration velocity found in this study was 
lower than the recommendation provided in the inter national standards of vibration effect on 
historical structure.
