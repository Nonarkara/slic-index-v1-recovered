National Steering Committee chaired by

modern cities capitalizing on the use of 
technology and digital innovation to en -
hance the efficiency of city management 
and build a sustainable urban ecosystem. 
Aiming to improve the people’s quality 
of life, the proactive and concrete action 
plan is grounded in several factors, such

the readiness of resources, and the iden-
tity of each city. In doing so, the Ministry 
is considering robust methods, measures 
and mechanisms stemmed from productive 
synergy among sectors and long-term re-
lations with trusted international partners.

this “Smart City Thailand” model is showing 
strong signs of progress, especially among 
cities that have recently been endorsed 
as future smart cities. For instance, the 
development of a tourism revitalization 
plan in Phuket Province, known as the 
“Phuket Sandbox,” is a clear manifesta -

 The development of “Smart 
City” is one of the key themes of 
economic development and social 
transformation globally. In Thailand, 
Smart City refers to the mutual 
efforts and collaboration between 
the citizens—as the space owners, 
the government, and the private 
sector—to create a livable and 
modern city and enhance people’s 
quality of life. As a crucial driver of 
this initiative, the Digital Economy 
Promotion Agency (depa) has 
implemented the “Smart City Plan” 
and aligned it with the 20-year 
National Strategy. Such a plan aims 
to build 100 smart cities in Thailand 
and drive three of them to be inter-
nationally accredited smart cities. At 
the same time, the Ministry of Digital 
Economy and Society has vigorously 
worked towards building efficient 
digital infrastructure to cover all 
of Thailand. Furthermore, as part 
of Thailand’s 4.0 Digital Strategy, 
the development of smart cities is 
recognized as an essential 
apparatus of the Thai government to 
help reduce inequality and decen-
tralize prosperity to other areas. As 
such, Thailand Smart City Week 2020 
had been organized by taking 
into account the importance of 
connecting smart urban networks 
domestically and internationally. 
The purposes of the event were 
to navigate Thailand to becoming 
one of the future global smart 
cities, to bridge people and digital 
technology, thereby leading to 
sustainable economic development 
and social transformation. Last but 
not least, the event would manifest the 
persistent commitment and 
promptness of Thailand and its 
government in moving, with 
confidence and ample investment 
opportunities, towards the digital 
age.

An Overview of Thailand 
Smart City Week 2020 

 The “Thailand Smart City Week 
2020” is the first mega event that pro -
motes smart city technologies and 
the first opportunity for Thai people to 
experience a REAL Digital Event in the 
field of Smart City. The event, which took 
place between October 16 to 22, 2020, 
aimed to drive smart city initiatives, make 
smart-city lifestyles more accessible, and 
encourage people to become vital drivers 
of smart cities. The Ministry of Digital 
Economy and Society, as well as the 
Digital Economy Promotion Agency 
(depa), play critical roles in steering 
Thailand toward Smart City development.
 Thailand take advantage of modern 
technology and digital innovation, 
improve service efficiency and city 
management system, and improve 
people’s living quality under a modern 
and livable city paradigm. The success 
of this event is a milestone result of the 
mutual efforts between governmental 
organizations, the private sector, the 
education sector, and civic engagement. 
Furthermore, it is the first time that 
Thailand is focusing on developing a 
concrete idea of smart cities, which 
is stemmed from the REAL Solutions 
for REAL People in REAL Smart Cities 
concept.

Thailand Smart City 
Week 2020’s Success
 Throughout the event, Thai -
land Smart City Week 2020 offered 
thought-provoking awareness of 
smart city development in Thailand 
to over 300,000 people, in turn 
generating more than 300 million 
baht from business negotiations. 
This has led to depa Thailand 
continuing with its excellent plan 
to promote the development of 
smart cities in 2021 through the 
“Smart City Ambassadors” initiative. 
This program has selected over 
30 young individuals who have 
unique connections with their 
hometown and come from cities 
actively promoting smart city 
development. The main objective 
of such a selection is for talents 
from 30 cities across 26 provinces 
in Thailand to promote and enhance 
any essential skills required for smart 
and sustainable urban development. 
These activities will be initiated with 
representatives of city development 
agencies or the so-called “Captains.”
 The key activity is “Bootcamp,” 
a one-year program that promotes 
livable smart cities through intensive 
 masterclasses and fieldwork. The 
Bootcamp also aims to improve 
different dimensions of the cities 
in order to boost the quality of 
life of the people and bring for- 
ward innovative solutions to city 
problems. In doing so, the project 
will be supported by a network of 
digital startups and technology 
service providers. These providers 
will be involved either in the tech -
nological change for over 200 
communities or no less than 2,000 
business technology projects and 
the upskilling of the workforce, such 
as building new careers in artificial 
intelligence (A.I) and drone techno- 
logy. Besides, the providers will help 
develop at least ten infrastructure 
projects in health services, tourism, 
transportation, public security, and 
the City Data Platform. As a result, 
investments valued at more than 
500 million baht between the public 
and the private sectors will be 
contributed towards this project.

Why should you “experience” 
a smart city?
 What difficulties or complications 
do you frequently face in your city these 
days? Whether it is a pollution problem, 
travel inconvenience, or waste and 
environmental problems, it is hard to 
deny that the more populated the city, 
the more “problems” will follow. Today, 
the word “smart” seems to offer an 
alternative for handling these collective 
city issues. “Smart” refers to using digital 
innovation and technology to help solve 
problems in a faster and more convenient 
way. With new management approaches, 
such technology has become a crucial 
and effective mechanism in handling and 
solving city problems. One realization that 
may contribute to the smart city develop-
ment is that it is not only an obligation of 
government agencies or local authorities 
to carry out initiatives; instead, it is the 
mutual efforts of the private sector and 
the public that are of tremendous impor-
tance. Moreover, every sector should be 
resilient in its efforts to create a culture 
of openness to ideas, experimentation, 
and innovative digital technology. Besides, 
new management and investment models 
will eventually engender the sharp and 
concrete development of smart cities. 
Regardless of who you are, wealth, status, 
or career paths, every human is entitled to 
a good life with less social inequality and 
disparity. Achieving a good life may not

1. CU Centenary Park

Luang-Samyan area. The Park is covered

species of animals. Also found in the Chula 
Centenary Park, the famous pond acts as 
a recycling reservoir that redirects water 
purified by the wastewater management 
process at the CU Terrace & CU iHouse 
building to water the plants in the park. 
Furthermore, the air-quality monitoring 
devices will ensure that everyone can enjoy 
clean air while exercising as the air quality 
real-time monitoring and pollution detec-
tion systems give them both the peace of 
mind as well as the data-driven decision.

ages.

4 Questions with Smart City 
HACKs Advisor,

 When we try to solve a city problem, 
there are usually many stakeholders 
involved. So getting to the root of the 
problem is key. For example, when 
I was working on a project to reduce 
traffic accidents in a city. Our first 
job was to understand the nature of 
those accidents — these would usually 
include the cause, the people involved, 
the location, the time, and so on. Once 
these details have been established, we 
stepped back to look at the bigger picture 
to identify the heat map of traffic accident 
in that particular city. Perhaps we could 
prioritize them so that we utilize the available 
resources in the best possible way.
 Then we explored what solutions 
were available to reduce or eliminate 
accidents at these top priority locations. 
In this particular example, we identified 
10 locations and 5 causes — namely, red 
light running, bikers not wearing a helmet, 
illegal parking, not travelling on the proper 
traffic lane — or driving/riding in the 
opposite direction to the traffic flow, 
and last but not least, speeding. At each 
location, we installed different types of 
sensors to detect these traffic violations. 
And we implemented an automated 
traffic ticketing system where all the law 
enforcement officers need to do was to 
confirm the violations on the computer 
at the end of each day before printing out 
the tickets and sending them through the 
post. We all thought this would bring down 
the traffic accidents significantly. But 
what we didn’t realize was that there was 
a law which states that each traffic ticket 
must be sent by a specially registered 
post and this cost more money. After 3 
months of running the automated traffic 
ticketing system, the law enforcement 
offices ran out of money to pay for the 
postage.

 Smart City Development should not be framed or sought 
by the best technology or a one-size-fits-all model to address 
urban problems. Instead, such a development should start 
from exploring the actual issues of the people in the area and 
examining the city’s context. At times, being smart does not 
necessarily mean having clear boundaries around the city. Being 
smart can also be applied to one road or twenty-five families in 
a community. However, the most important thing is to learn, 
explore, and understand the people’s real problems before 
searching for any solutions or applying them without knowing 
the potential contexts of the community. Thus, it is vital to un -
derstand a smart city from the eyes of those who live in the 
community. 
 Cities are made up of people. A city is made up of many 
people who live together and participate in various activities. So, 
what constitutes a “Smart City?” First, you must investigate the 
issues that a specific group of people faces, because even the 
best and most innovative technology may not be designed to 
address specific issues or meet everyone’s needs. However, what 
everyone requires is a tool that can help alleviate or resolve

The Importance of Cities and 
Innovation Development 
 According to the United Nations, 
a “city” is recently redefined as an area 
where more than 55 percent of the world’s 
inhabitants reside. The cause of this change 
in definition is a result of the change in 
economic systems, particularly the re -
placement of traditional agriculture and 
manufacturing sectors with the services 
sector that generates higher economic 
value. Thanks to this, the economy has 
grown by leaps and bounds. Furthermore, 
coupled with technological advancements, 
machines and automation can now, in 
some part, replace the work of humans 
with higher precision and better efficiency 
than human labor in agriculture. As a result, 
the traditional manufacturing sector is 
dwindling. Both contexts are the push and 
pull factors that cause the movement of 
urban migration for severala number of 
reasons, particularly working, study -
ing, or residing. As a result, this forms 
the centralization of infrastructure, 
economic, social, and cultural invest -
ments of the urban areas—all of which 
can be denoted by the density, com -
pactness, and cultural diversity. 
 This “urbanity” is what many 
thinkers and researchers regard as 
the source of creativity, an essential 
element of innovation, particularly of 
technological change. Provided that 
a city is an area filled with expertise, 
common problems, density, and space 
constraints, ideas and interests are 
being gathered and transformed into 
solutions, which are the main driving 
force of the economy or the so-called 
innovative ecosystem. Undoubtedly, 
the continuous development of inno -
